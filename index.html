Models & Fields

01 — The Three Charges

Matter has three qualities

Matter accelerates according to the force field it is immersed in. All matter has inertial charge (mass) and so accelerates in a gravitational field. Some matter additionally has electric charge, and some responds to magnetic fields too.

The deep structure here is a mapping: each type of field defines a space in which objects experience a force proportional to a specific property of the object. The pattern repeats across all three fundamental interactions accessible at the IB scale.

02 — Ontology of Fields

Nouns or verbs?

When we draw those arrowed lines radiating from a charge or a planet, what are they? Are they things that exist in space, or processes that describe what would happen to a test particle placed there?

This is a question of ontology: the branch of philosophy concerned with what exists. Three competing pictures have shaped the history of physics.

The 19th-century view: space is filled with a luminiferous aether, a material substance through which fields propagate as strains and stresses. Michelson and Morley's null result (1887) undermined this picture, but the intuition that "something is there" never quite disappeared.

Faraday and Maxwell's view, which dominates modern physics: the field is a real entity that exists at every point in space, carrying energy and momentum. It doesn't need a medium. The field is the medium. It is a noun.

A more cautious reading: the field is a mathematical description of how test particles would accelerate. It tells us what would happen, not what is there. On this view, field lines are verbs: "if you placed a charge here, it would accelerate that way." The field is a prediction, not a substance.

The Forest Question

Does the Earth's gravitational field exist if there is no test mass that accelerates due to it? The consensus in modern physics: yes. The field carries energy (gravitational waves have been detected by LIGO) and exists independent of being measured. We don't need to put in test balls. But this answer itself is a philosophical commitment.

03 — Gravitational Fields

From radial to uniform

Earth's gravitational field is radial: it points inward from every direction, with strength falling as $$g \propto \frac{1}{r^2}$$ outside the surface. But near the surface, on a scale where the Earth looks flat, the field lines are approximately parallel and the strength approximately constant: $$g \approx 9.81\;\text{ms}^{-2}$$

This is why projectile problems work with independent horizontal and vertical components: the uniform-field approximation means the vertical acceleration doesn't depend on horizontal position.

Interactive: g(r) inside and outside Earth

Outside Earth

$$g = G\frac{M}{r^2}$$

The field strength falls with the inverse square of distance. The density of field lines through a spherical shell at radius $r$ goes as $1/r^2$ because the shell area goes as $4\pi r^2$. This is geometry, not a force law.

Inside Earth

$$g \propto r$$

Only the mass at radii smaller than your position contributes (shell theorem). As you descend, enclosed mass shrinks as $r^3$ while the $1/r^2$ grows, net result: $g$ grows linearly. This linear restoring force means a ball dropped through a tunnel would oscillate with simple harmonic motion.

04 — Electric Fields

Coulomb's architecture

The electric field has the same $1/r^2$ radial structure as gravity, but with a crucial difference: charge comes in two signs. This means electric fields can be attractive or repulsive, and the superposition of multiple charges creates rich, complex field patterns.

Interactive: drag charges to rearrange the field

Field and potential

The electric field $\vec{E}$ tells you the force per unit charge: $\vec{E} = \vec{F}/q$. The electric potential $V$ tells you the energy per unit charge: $V = U/q$. They are related by $$\vec{E} = -\nabla V = -\frac{dV}{dr}\hat{r}$$ Field lines are always perpendicular to equipotential surfaces. Where field lines are dense, the potential changes rapidly.

Key Definitions

Electric field strength $E$: force per unit positive test charge.
Potential difference $\Delta V$: work done per unit charge moving between two points.
Absolute potential $V$: work done per unit charge bringing a test charge from infinity.
Work done: $W = q\Delta V$

04b — Uniform Electric Fields

Parallel plates: gravity for charges

Between two large parallel conducting plates with a potential difference $V$ separated by distance $d$, the electric field is uniform: $$E = \frac{V}{d}$$ This is the electric analogue of the uniform gravitational field near Earth's surface. A charged particle entering horizontally between the plates experiences a constant vertical force $F = qE$, producing a parabolic trajectory -- exactly like a projectile in a gravitational field.

The horizontal velocity is unchanged (no horizontal force), while the vertical acceleration is $$a = \frac{qE}{m} = \frac{qV}{md}$$ The mathematics is identical to projectile motion: replace $g$ with $qE/m$.

The analogy with projectiles

In a gravitational field, a ball thrown horizontally follows $y = \frac{1}{2}g\!\left(\frac{x}{v_0}\right)^{\!2}$. Between parallel plates, an electron entering horizontally follows $y = \frac{1}{2}\frac{qE}{m}\!\left(\frac{x}{v_0}\right)^{\!2}$. The parabola is identical in structure -- only the "effective g" changes.

This is the principle behind the cathode ray tube: electrons are accelerated by an electron gun, then deflected vertically by parallel plates to sweep a beam across a phosphor screen.

After leaving the plates

Once the electron exits the plates, the field vanishes and it travels in a straight line at whatever velocity (and direction) it had at the exit point. The total deflection on a distant screen depends on both the in-plate parabola and the post-plate straight-line projection.

This is exactly like a ball leaving a table edge: parabolic while the force acts, then straight-line once it stops.

Key Result

Vertical deflection inside plates of length $L$: $\;y = \frac{qEL^2}{2mv_0^2}$
Exit angle: $\;\tan\theta = \frac{qEL}{mv_0^2}$
These are directly analogous to projectile range and angle expressions with $g \to qE/m$.

04c — Crossed Fields

The velocity selector

What happens when we apply both an electric field and a magnetic field at right angles to each other, and to the velocity of a charged particle? The electric force $qE$ pushes in one direction; the magnetic force $qvB$ pushes in the opposite direction. They balance when: $$qE = qvB \implies v = \frac{E}{B}$$

Only particles with this exact speed travel straight through. Faster particles curve one way, slower particles curve the other. The charge cancels: the selected speed is independent of $q$ and $m$. This is the velocity selector, and it was the key to Thomson's measurement of $e/m$.

Thomson's two-step method

Step 1 -- find the speed: Cross E and B fields until the beam is undeflected. Then $v = E/B$.

Step 2 -- find e/m: Switch off the E field. The beam now curves in the B field alone with radius $r = mv/(qB)$. Measuring $r$ gives $$\frac{e}{m} = \frac{v}{rB} = \frac{E}{rB^2}$$

Thomson's result: $e/m \approx 1.76 \times 10^{11}\;\text{C/kg}$, roughly 1800 times larger than for hydrogen ions. The electron was far lighter than any atom.

Why this matters

The velocity selector demonstrates that E and B fields are not independent phenomena -- they are two aspects of a single electromagnetic field. The fact that $v = E/B$ is independent of charge and mass makes it a universal speed filter, used today in mass spectrometers and particle accelerators.

05 — Magnetic Fields & Charged Particles

The perpendicular force

The magnetic force is unlike gravity or electricity: it acts perpendicular to both the velocity and the field. This means it never does work (it cannot speed up or slow down the particle), but it curves the trajectory into a circle. For a charge $q$ moving at speed $v$ in a uniform field $B$:

$$F = qvB = \frac{mv^2}{r} \implies r = \frac{mv}{qB}$$

The radius of curvature directly encodes the momentum: $p = mv = qBr$. This is how particle physicists measure momentum from bubble chamber tracks.

Simulation: charged particle in a uniform B field (into page)

Thomson's experiment

In 1897, J.J. Thomson used crossed electric and magnetic fields to measure the charge-to-mass ratio $e/m$ of cathode rays. By balancing the electric deflection $qE$ against the magnetic deflection $qvB$, he could find the speed: $v = E/B$. Then the radius of the circular path in the magnetic field alone gave $e/m = v/(rB)$. The result: cathode rays were particles 1800 times lighter than hydrogen, and universal across all cathode materials. The electron had been discovered.

Track Momentum

For a particle track with radius $r = 2.3\;\text{m}$ in a known magnetic field, $p = qBr$. At particle colliders, this is how we measure momentum. A shrinking radius means the particle is losing energy (e.g. via synchrotron radiation), giving a spiral rather than a circle.

06 — The Observer

Are we spinning, or is the universe spinning around us?

This is not a rhetorical question. It touches the deepest issues in the foundations of physics: what is motion relative to?

Newton's Bucket: what does it tell us about absolute rotation?
+

Newton imagined a bucket of water, spinning. Initially the water is flat while the bucket spins. Later, the water has caught up and is concave. The concavity isn't relative to the bucket (the relative motion has stopped). Newton concluded that the water "knows" it is spinning relative to absolute space.

Mach's Principle: is the answer in the distant stars?
+

Ernst Mach proposed that inertia arises from the gravitational influence of all the matter in the universe. Your dizziness when you spin is due to your rotation relative to the most causally connected distant galaxies. If they were spinning and you were not, you would feel nothing. Einstein was deeply influenced by this idea when developing general relativity, though GR does not fully incorporate Mach's principle.

Leibnizian Relationism: does it matter who is spinning?
+

Leibniz argued that space is nothing but the relations between objects. There is no "absolute" space to be spinning relative to. Only relative motion exists. This is the most radical position: you spinning and the universe spinning around you are literally the same physical situation described in two equivalent ways.

"The observable phenomena depend only on the relative motion of the conductor and the magnet, whereas the customary view draws a sharp distinction between the two cases in which one or the other of the two bodies is in motion." — Albert Einstein, On the Electrodynamics of Moving Bodies (1905)

Einstein's opening sentence of the special relativity paper is itself about fields and observers. Whether you move a magnet past a conductor, or move the conductor through the magnet's field, the observable result (an induced current) is the same. The asymmetry is in the model, not in nature.

Foucault's Pendulum

One experiment that can distinguish Earth's rotation from the universe spinning around us: a Foucault pendulum. Its plane of oscillation rotates relative to the floor at a rate that depends on latitude. This is consistent with Earth spinning, and would require an absurdly conspiratorial arrangement if the universe were revolving around a stationary Earth.

How does the sun's oscillation through the galactic plane affect Earth's history?
+

The Sun oscillates above and below the galactic plane with a period of roughly 60 million years. Some researchers have proposed correlations between these passages and mass extinction events, because the denser midplane increases the flux of cosmic rays and may gravitationally perturb the Oort cloud, sending comets toward the inner solar system. The evidence is debated but the question itself beautifully connects SHM, galactic dynamics, and the history of life.

What do Milankovitch cycles say about Earth's changing orbit?
+

Three orbital parameters oscillate on long timescales: eccentricity (~100k and ~400k years), obliquity (~41k years), and precession (~26k years). Together they modulate the distribution of solar energy by latitude and season, driving ice age cycles. Each is an example of oscillatory or circular motion in a gravitational field, shaped by perturbations from other planets.

How can Doppler shifting reveal an orbiting exoplanet?
+

A planet doesn't orbit a stationary star; both orbit their common centre of mass. As the star wobbles toward us, its spectral lines blueshift; as it wobbles away, they redshift. The periodicity reveals the orbital period, and the amplitude reveals the planet's mass (up to the unknown inclination). This is the radial velocity method, responsible for the first confirmed exoplanet detections.

How are line absorption spectra the "fingerprints" of distant stars?
+

Each element absorbs photons at specific wavelengths determined by its electron energy levels. When starlight passes through a star's outer atmosphere, those wavelengths are absorbed, leaving dark lines in the continuous spectrum. The pattern is unique to each element. By matching observed absorption lines to laboratory measurements, astronomers identify chemical composition across billions of light-years.
